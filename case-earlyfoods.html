01 · Problem

Every food e-commerce site is optimised for sales.
None are designed for trust.

Organic baby food is a category where the buyer and the end user are different people. A parent choosing what their infant eats is not making a convenience decision — it is a safety decision, made repeatedly, under pressure, with imperfect information.

EarlyFoods' founder understood this instinctively: the brand existed because she could not find food she trusted for her own children. The design brief was unusual for e-commerce: put trust before conversion at every touchpoint. Do not rush the parent to checkout. Make them feel safe first.

"If a parent has to wonder whether this is safe, the design already failed."
— Design principle, EarlyFoods

02 · Research

400+ parent interviews. Empathy mapping. Understanding the anxiety of feeding.

Research began with conversations with the founder — understanding her own journey from frustrated parent to entrepreneur. Then 400+ interviews with parents across the UAE: Indian expats, Emirati families, Western professionals. The anxiety of feeding a child chemical-free food in a market dominated by processed options was universal.

Empathy mapping revealed a consistent emotional pattern: parents wanted certainty before commitment. They read ingredients obsessively, looked for certifications, sought reviews from people like them, and abandoned carts when anything felt ambiguous. The competitor landscape was optimising for conversion — precisely the wrong answer for this audience.

Finding 01

Parents read everything

Ingredient lists, certifications, sourcing stories, founder backgrounds. Every detail was scrutinised. Omitting information created distrust — it did not simplify.

Finding 02

Reviews from similar families matter most

UAE parents trusted reviews from people who shared their dietary context — Indian expats, Muslim families with halal requirements, families with allergy concerns.

Finding 03

Checkout anxiety is real

Parents abandoned carts not because of price, but because of unresolved doubt. Trust signals at checkout — not just at product pages — were critical.

03 · Design

Brand, information architecture, and a trust-first e-commerce system.

The design process covered three interconnected layers: brand identity, information architecture, and the e-commerce UX. All three needed to speak the same language — warmth, transparency, and confidence without arrogance.

The brand system was built around organic and natural motifs — a soft, earthy palette drawn from the actual ingredients (turmeric, spinach, beetroot), with typography that felt approachable rather than clinical. The logo combined a leaf and an early sun — growth, nourishment, first light.

01 / Brand

Identity from ingredients

Colour palette derived from actual product ingredients. Typography chosen for warmth over authority. Logo mark combining growth and nourishment.

02 / Architecture

Trust before discovery

Restructured the IA so that certifications, sourcing information, and founder story appeared before product listings — not buried in footers.

03 / Product UX

Transparent product pages

Full ingredient breakdowns with explanations, allergen highlights, age guidance, preparation methods, and sourcing provenance on every product page.

04 / Checkout

Reassurance at every step

Trust signals placed throughout the checkout flow — not just at cart. Certification badges, quality guarantees, and return policy visible at the moment of payment.

04 · Outcome

A brand parents trust.
An e-commerce that feels safe to buy from.

EarlyFoods launched in the UAE with a brand system and e-commerce experience built entirely around parental trust. Transparency was not a feature — it was the UX paradigm. Every information gap that caused cart abandonment in research was addressed by design. That same standard extended to accessibility: a brand built around informing parents cannot have information that some parents cannot reach. WCAG AA compliance was applied across every product page, checkout flow, and content component — because trust and inclusivity are the same obligation.

The project demonstrated that emotional UX is not soft — it is precise. Knowing which emotions to design for (safety, confidence, belonging to a community of informed parents) and which to avoid (urgency, scarcity, FOMO) required as much rigour as any conversion optimisation project. The difference: parents came back — not because they were nudged to, but because they felt safe.
