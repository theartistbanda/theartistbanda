§ 01 · Problem

The apps meant to save us from apps had become more of the same.

Screen-time dashboards shame. Blockers punish. Detox apps gamify abstinence, which is just another kind of compulsion. We were designing against our own industry's playbook, which meant the whole vocabulary of interventions had to change.

YourHour needed to help a billion-user context — an India where the smartphone is often the household's only computer — reclaim attention without turning usage control into another thing to feel guilty about.

"If the fix for a compulsion is another compulsion, you haven't designed. You've redecorated."
— Internal design principle, YourHour v2
§ 02 · Approach

Behaviour first.
Visuals second. Always.

01 / Research

300+ interviews across 4 cities

Mapped the actual moments of phone-reach: boredom, social anxiety, waiting, bedtime. Most were not entertainment — they were avoidance.

02 / Frame

Compassion, not control

Reframed the product from "blocker" to "coach". Copy, colour and interaction patterns all derived from this single pivot.

03 / Prototype

Gentle interventions

Soft nudges, breathing screens, and an intentional micro-delay before opening flagged apps. Friction measured in milliseconds, not minutes.

04 / Ship

22 languages, one tone

Localisation treated as design, not translation. Tone kits per language; copy approved by native speakers, not machines.

§ 03 · Outcome

Ten million users.
4.7 stars. Seventy thousand reviews.

The app shipped globally on Android and iOS, scaled past ten million installs, and settled at 4.7★ with over seventy thousand reviews. The ratings that matter most are the qualitative ones — people describing it as the first one of these that did not make me feel worse.

The behavioural deltas: measurable reductions in nightly phone reach, higher completion on user-set goals, and retention curves that flatten rather than churn — the opposite of what engagement-maxed products produce.
